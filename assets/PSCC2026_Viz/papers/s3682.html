A Distributed High-frequency Model of a Large-scale Intershielded Transformer Winding to Study Electrical Faults

I. MOTIVATION In an electric power system, transformers are crucial, expensive components, and their failure can cause serious problems. In the insulation system of transformers, the winding insulation is the most vulnerable component to faults [1]. To ensure reliable transformer operation, fault identification is essential. To gain a better understanding of fault propagation within the windings, high-frequency modeling of the transformer windings is inevitable. The model can also be used to simulate and analyze different scenarios and conditions of transformer windings whether in healthy or faulty conditions. II. NOVEL CONTRIBUTIONS This work presents a transformer winding simulation model and verifies it experimentally. To accurately simulate the propagation of faults along the winding, an axial-multiconductor transmission line (AMTL) model of the winding is employed [2]. The AMTL model takes the variation in the radius of winding turns into account, providing a more accurate representation at high frequencies. The model is applied to a high-voltage continuous disk intershielded transformer winding with 42 disks [3]. The modelled case comprehensively analyzes the proposed model’s performance for such complicated winding configurations. The verifications are conducted using various techniques and measurements up to a few MHz frequency, demonstrating the accuracy and reliability of the AMTL model in transformer windings. The developed model can be used to simulate transient or nontransient electrical faults in transformer windings to develop a condition monitoring system. III. THE PROPOSED APPROACH The AMTL theory is presented and applied to the 42-disk test transformer winding introduced above. Calculating the per-unit length (PUL) parameters of the windings is also discussed. Finite element method (FEM) is employed to calculate PUL capacitance and inductance matrices of the transformer winding. They are calculated based on the numerical solution of Maxwell’s equations in electrostatic and magnetostatic regimes, respectively. Frequency-dependent resistance and conductance are calculated based on the cross-sectional shape of the conductors to model losses in the winding. There are several shield turns in the first few disks of the winding. In order to model the shield turns, they are considered as conductors of the AMTL model with their own dimensions and configurations. IV. NUMERICAL EXPERIMENTS AND VALIDATION Model verification is achieved by comparing a measured input admittance using an Omicron Bode Analyzer with the simulated input admittance calculated using the winding models, considering different resistance calculations. To evaluate the accuracy of the proposed model for fault detection, experiments are conducted. The experiments evaluates the model performance for the two common types of electrical faults in transformer windings, which are partial discharge and short circuit. REFERENCES [1] M. R. Hussain, S.
